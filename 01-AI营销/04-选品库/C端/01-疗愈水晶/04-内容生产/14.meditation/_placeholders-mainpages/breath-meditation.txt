<p>Breath meditation, formally recognized as <strong>anapanasati</strong>, is a structured practice of mindfulness focused on tracking the natural rhythm of inhalation and exhalation. Rooted in ancient contemplative frameworks, this method requires no special equipment—only a quiet space and consistent attention. Many practitioners use the steady cadence of breathing as a reliable <strong>mindful anchor</strong> to stabilize wandering thoughts, making it one of the most accessible techniques for building sustained focus.</p>

@@@AI_MEANING_DEEP@@@
<p>In the <strong>Theravada tradition</strong>, <em>(南传上座部：anapanasati (安那般那念) 是佛陀教导的核心止观法门)</em> serves as a foundational practice for developing <strong>shamatha</strong> (止) concentration and <strong>vipassana</strong> (观) insight. The technique follows a precise sequence designed to train attention without forcing control:</p>
<p>1. Sit and let breath flow naturally</p>
<p>2. Notice the sensation at nostrils or belly</p>
<p>3. Count breaths 1-10 if helpful, then restart</p>
<p>4. When distracted, return without judgment</p>
<p>5. Rest in bare awareness of breathing</p>
<p>This framework encourages observers to track physiological feedback rather than chasing mental stillness. When the mind wanders, the practitioner simply notes the drift and guides attention back to the physical act of breathing. Over time, this repeated redirection strengthens neural pathways associated with executive control and emotional regulation.</p>

When placed near a meditation cushion, many people find that the stone’s clarity supports a <strong>mindfulness application</strong> focused on maintaining uninterrupted attention on the <strong>inhalation and exhalation cycle</strong>, helping to reduce visual fatigue during extended <strong>shamatha</strong> sessions.</p>
