<p><strong>walking meditation</strong> is a foundational contemplative practice that transforms ordinary movement into a structured attention exercise. Defined in its earliest texts as the deliberate alignment of breath and step, this method invites practitioners to anchor awareness in the present moment through physical motion. Rather than treating the body as a distraction, the practice uses gait, posture, and rhythm as direct objects of observation. Many people find that pairing this technique with a steady tactile object creates a consistent sensory reference point. The approach remains accessible whether practiced indoors or outdoors, requiring only a clear space and a willingness to slow down.</p>

<p>1. Choose a quiet path (10-20 paces)<br>2. Walk slowly, noting 'lifting, moving, placing'<br>3. Match breath to steps if comfortable<br>4. Turn mindfully at the end<br>5. Keep eyes soft-focused</p>

The turn requires the same deliberate awareness as forward motion, preventing the mind from rushing ahead. Maintaining a soft visual field reduces sensory overload, allowing internal focus to deepen. This sequence aligns with traditional monastic routines that prioritize consistency over intensity.</p>
