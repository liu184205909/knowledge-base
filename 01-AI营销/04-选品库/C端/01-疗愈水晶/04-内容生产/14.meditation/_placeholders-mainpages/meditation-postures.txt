@@@AI_MEANING_DEEP@@@
<p>Physical alignment shapes mental clarity. In 禅宗坐禅 (Zen seated meditation), the spine acts as a vertical axis, while 瑜伽 asana emphasizes joint stacking to prevent strain. These cross-traditional guidelines converge on one principle: meditation postures should feel secure without becoming rigid. Follow these specific arrangements to build your foundation:</p>
<p><strong>1. Cross-legged on cushion (burmese/half-lotus)</strong> — Sit on a firm cushion. Tilt the pelvis forward until both sit bones press evenly into the seat. Take five slow cycles through the nose, imagining the base of the spine as a solid mountain root.</p>
<p><strong>2. Seiza kneeling with bench</strong> — Kneel on a soft mat and rest the thighs on a low bench. Draw the tailbone downward slightly. Breathe in for four counts, out for six, visualizing gravity flowing straight down through the kneecaps like water.</p>
<p><strong>3. Chair: feet flat, spine tall</strong> — Choose a straight-back chair. Plant both feet hip-width apart. Lengthen the crown upward while dropping the collarbones. Inhale for five seconds, exhale for seven, picturing a wooden pole standing freely in still air.</p>
<p><strong>4. Lying (savasana) for body practices</strong> — Extend onto a firm floor or bed. Bend the knees slightly, then release them outward. Rest the arms at forty-five degrees. Count to ten as you exhale, feeling the body settle like a stone sinking into warm sand.</p>
<p><strong>5. Key: spine tall, shoulders relaxed, alert-yet-easy</strong> — Maintain this balance throughout. Adjust only when sharp pain appears.</p>
<p><em>Note: Cross-traditional posture principles prioritize sustainability over rigidity, allowing natural micro-movements to dissipate static fatigue.</em></p>
