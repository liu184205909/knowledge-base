@@@AI_HUB_VSWESTERN@@@
The simplest way to hold the difference: the Western zodiac divides the year by month (12 constellations the sun passes through), while the Chinese zodiac divides it by year (12 animals, one per year, cycling every twelve years). They're two different lenses — one cultural-symbolic, one astrological — and they don't map cleanly onto each other. Someone born in June 1990 is a Gemini in Western astrology and a Horse in the Chinese zodiac; both readings are valid, and many people find reading both gives a richer picture than either alone. The stones paired with each system are different too: Western zodiac crystals tend to follow the four-element framework, while Chinese zodiac crystals follow the Five Elements (Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water) and traditional compatibility pairings.

@@@AI_HUB_BMN_INTRO@@@
Ben ming nian (本命年) is your own zodiac year — the year that comes around every twelve years when the animal of the year matches your birth animal. Across Chinese zodiac tradition, ben ming nian is read as a year of change and amplified visibility rather than pure smooth sailing; the folk framing is that you "offend Tai Sui" (犯太岁) in your own year, which is a symbolic way of saying the year tends to surface shifts and tests. This is not a prediction of bad luck. It's a cultural framing that has led many people, for centuries, to wear a protective stone throughout their ben ming nian as a symbolic anchor. In 2026, Horses are in their ben ming nian; in 2025 it was Snakes; in 2027 it will be Goats.

@@@AI_HUB_FAQ_VS@@@
The Western zodiac assigns signs by birth month based on the constellations the sun passes through. The Chinese zodiac assigns signs by birth year based on a 12-year animal cycle, paired with one of five elements that rotates on a slower cycle. They're entirely different systems — different sign sets, different element frameworks (4 vs 5), different ruling logic (month vs year). Both are valid lenses for self-reflection. Many people read both their Western and Chinese signs; the two tend to illuminate different facets, and neither cancels the other out.
