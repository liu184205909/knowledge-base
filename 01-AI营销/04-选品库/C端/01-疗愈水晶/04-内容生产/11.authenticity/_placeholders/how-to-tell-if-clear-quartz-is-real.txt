@@@AI_INTRODUCTION@@@
Clear quartz is the crystal most often copied in glass — and the one where buyers get most confused, because the word "crystal" itself means two different things. A wine glass is "crystal" in the tableware sense (leaded glass); a clear quartz point is "crystal" in the mineral sense (a natural silicon dioxide crystal). This guide walks through six tests that separate genuine clear quartz from glass, reconstituted material, and the naming traps that surround it.

@@@AI_M1_INTRO@@@
Genuine clear quartz is silicon dioxide (SiO₂, Mohs 7, specific gravity 2.65) — a natural mineral with an ordered crystalline lattice. Glass is an amorphous solid with a disordered structure. That single structural difference drives every test: quartz conducts heat faster (it feels cool and stays cool), scratches harder (Mohs 7 versus glass's 5.5), traps no round bubbles, and bends light at a measurable angle (refractive index 1.544). The tests below read those differences.

@@@AI_M1_TEST_TEMPERATURE@@@
Temperature — genuine clear quartz feels cool and stays cool; glass warms quickly in your hand.

@@@AI_M1_TEST_HARDNESS_OR_BUBBLES@@@
Bubbles under a 10x loupe — round air bubbles mean glass; natural clear quartz shows irregular inclusions (needles, veils) or is inclusion-free.

@@@AI_M1_TEST_PRICE_NAMING@@@
The naming trap — "crystal quartz" sold cheaply is often glass. Natural quartz is properly called "quartz," and reputable sellers name it clearly.

@@@AI_M2_PARA1_MARKET@@@
Clear quartz is the most-imitated crystal for an unfortunate reason: it is genuinely affordable, so the fakes do not even need to be sophisticated. A factory can produce clear glass beads by the thousand for less than the cost of cutting genuine quartz, and visually they are similar enough at a glance to pass in cheap jewelry and bulk listings. The volume is enormous — clear quartz is used in beads, points, clusters, spheres, and wands — and the further a buyer is from the source, the easier glass substitution becomes.

@@@AI_M2_PARA2_FAKE_TYPES@@@
Clear quartz fakes fall into four recognizable tiers. The first and most common is ordinary glass — cheap, often with round bubbles, sold as beads or small points. The second is reconstituted quartz: natural quartz powder melted down with glass and re-formed, which sits in a gray zone (it contains some real quartz but is not a natural crystal). The third is synthetic lab-grown quartz — chemically genuine SiO₂ but factory-made, usually too perfect and inclusion-free. The fourth is the naming trap: leaded glass ("lead crystal," "crystal glass") sold using the tableware industry's word "crystal," which has nothing to do with natural mineral crystals.

@@@AI_M2_PARA3_WHY_HARD@@@
Clear quartz is harder to authenticate than amethyst, because its defining feature — clarity — is exactly what glass copies best. There is no color zoning to read, no natural purple gradient to compare. So the tests look past the surface, at what is inside (bubbles versus inclusions), how the stone behaves (temperature, hardness), and how heavy it feels (density). The good news is that glass is usually sloppy: it traps bubbles, it is the wrong density, and it cannot scratch true glass. The tests are reliable when you run them together.

@@@AI_M3_METHODS_INTRO@@@
These six tests are ordered from least destructive to confirmatory. The bubble check is the headline — it catches most glass fakes in seconds. Hardness confirms the quartz family on rough stones only. Temperature and heft are quick non-destructive signals. The refractive index is the professional measurement. And the naming test reads the listing itself, which is where many fakes reveal themselves before you even touch the stone.

@@@AI_M4_INTRO@@@
Clear quartz's mineral profile anchors every test. The key practical question is usually which of the three tiers you are holding — natural, reconstituted, or pure glass — so here is the quick reference, then how to tell them apart.

@@@AI_M5_INTRO@@@
Most clear quartz tests need only a $10 loupe and a piece of ordinary glass. A few additional tools help, and for valuable large clusters or cathedral points, a gemological lab settles questions home tests cannot.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_LOUPE@@@
A 10x jeweler's loupe is the essential tool for clear quartz. It reveals the single most diagnostic feature: whether the stone contains round bubbles (glass) or natural inclusions (needles, veils, mineral flecks) characteristic of genuine quartz. For any transparent stone sold as clear quartz, the loupe is where the answer lives.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_MOHS_KIT@@@
A Mohs hardness reference, or simply a glass jar, confirms clear quartz (Mohs 7) by its ability to scratch ordinary glass (Mohs 5.5). Use this only on rough or stones you do not mind marking — it is a destructive test. Never scratch-test a polished sphere, wand, or cluster you intend to keep pristine.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_UV_LIGHT@@@
A UV flashlight is less useful for clear quartz than for amethyst, since most clear quartz shows little fluorescence. It can still help distinguish some synthetic quartz (which may fluoresce differently) from natural, but it is a secondary tool here rather than a primary one.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_REFRACTOMETER@@@
A gemological refractometer measures clear quartz's refractive index precisely at 1.544, distinguishing it from glass (around 1.50) and from diamond simulants. This is a professional tool — most buyers will not own one, but it is the measurable, repeatable way to confirm "quartz" versus "glass" beyond the loupe.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_LAB_CERT@@@
For large or valuable clear quartz — museum-grade clusters, large cathedral points, specimen-quality spheres — a gemological laboratory report can confirm natural origin and distinguish natural from synthetic quartz through inclusion analysis. Most everyday clear quartz is inexpensive enough that home tests suffice; the lab matters for specimen-grade pieces.

@@@AI_M7_INTRO@@@
If the tests above tell you how to read clear quartz, this section tells you how to read the seller. Clear quartz is the most-imitated crystal in glass, so the listing itself often reveals whether you are looking at natural quartz or a glass substitute before you even touch the stone.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_1_NAMING@@@
Naming transparency. The listing names the stone as "Clear Quartz" or "Quartz (SiO₂)" — and crucially, not "crystal quartz" at a suspiciously low price, which often signals glass using the tableware industry's word "crystal." Treatments and reconstitution are disclosed. A seller who names clear quartz correctly avoids the naming trap on your behalf.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_2_ORIGIN@@@
Locality disclosure. The listing states a specific origin — Brazil, Madagascar, Arkansas (USA), or the Himalayas are common clear quartz sources. Specific origins signal a seller who knows their stock, and certain localities (Himalayan, Arkansas) are associated with collector interest.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_3_MINERAL_DATA@@@
Mineral data availability. The seller provides the Mohs hardness (7), specific gravity (2.65), or refractive index (1.544), or links to it. This lets you cross-check the home tests and signals a seller who understands mineralogy — the kind of seller less likely to stock glass labeled as quartz.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_4_RETURN_POLICY@@@
Clear return policy. A 30-day return policy lets you run the bubble check, temperature test, and (on rough stones) the hardness test at home, then return the clear quartz if it turns out to be glass. Sellers moving fakes resist returns; reputable sellers welcome them.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_5_REAL_PHOTOS@@@
Real photographs. The listing shows the actual clear quartz piece you will receive, with its natural inclusions or clarity character visible. Genuine clear quartz shows at least minor natural features and varies from piece to piece; mass-produced glass fakes look identical across listings and suspiciously perfect.

@@@AI_FAQ_9@@@
A rainbow inside clear quartz is usually a sign of natural character, not fakery. These internal rainbows come from tiny natural fractures that split light into spectral colors (sometimes called iris or rainbow quartz), or from interference by natural inclusions like rutile needles. Both are genuine features of natural quartz. A surface-applied, uniform iridescence on cheap "aura quartz" is a different matter — that is a metal coating, which is a legitimate treatment when disclosed (angel aura, aqua aura), but it is not the same as natural internal rainbows. Internal spectral flashes in an untreated clear quartz are a positive sign of natural origin.

@@@AI_FAQ_6@@@

It sits in a gray zone: it contains real quartz, so it shares some properties (it may scratch glass, it has roughly the right density), but it is not a natural crystal — it has been melted and re-formed industrially, so it lacks the natural crystal lattice and growth features of genuine clear quartz. Often it looks "too perfect" and may show faint flow lines from the manufacturing process. It should be disclosed as reconstituted, not sold as natural quartz.
