@@@AI_INTRODUCTION@@@
Amethyst is the crystal most people own first — and the one most likely to be faked. Its purple color is familiar, its popularity is enormous, and the gap between cheap glass imitations and genuine stone is wide enough to make substitution tempting. If you bought an amethyst point, a tumbled stone, or a beaded bracelet and want to know whether it is the real thing, this guide walks through seven tests that work specifically for amethyst, with the mineralogy behind each one.

@@@AI_M1_INTRO@@@
Real amethyst is a purple variety of quartz (SiO₂, Mohs 7) colored by iron and natural irradiation. Because it is quartz, it shares quartz's properties: it feels cool, it is hard enough to scratch glass, and under a 10x loupe it shows natural inclusions rather than round bubbles. The tests that catch amethyst fakes specifically look at color (is it naturally zoned or uniformly saturated?), inclusions (needles and two-phase pockets, or synthetic "breadcrumbs"?), and fluorescence (synthetic amethyst often glows blue under UV). Run them together and the picture gets clear.

@@@AI_M1_TEST_TEMPERATURE@@@
Temperature — genuine amethyst (quartz, Mohs 7) feels cool and stays cool in your hand, because minerals conduct heat faster than glass or plastic.

@@@AI_M1_TEST_HARDNESS_OR_BUBBLES@@@
Color zoning — natural amethyst shows subtle lighter and darker purple gradients, concentrated at the crystal tips. Synthetic amethyst looks uniformly saturated and "too perfect."

@@@AI_M1_TEST_PRICE_NAMING@@@
Price and naming — a large "natural amethyst" geode at the price of a tumbled stone is a red flag, and a listing that calls it "purple crystal" instead of naming the mineral deserves scrutiny.

@@@AI_M2_PARA1_MARKET@@@
Amethyst is the most faked crystal in the world for a straightforward reason: it is the most popular. High demand, finite natural supply, and a purple color that glass and lab processes can imitate closely create a strong incentive to substitute. Synthetic amethyst has been manufactured since the 1970s and is chemically identical to natural amethyst — same SiO₂, same Mohs 7, same density. Glass dyed purple is even cheaper. Together, these imitations fill a large share of the market, especially in inexpensive beads and small decorative pieces.

@@@AI_M2_PARA2_FAKE_TYPES@@@
The fakes fall into three main groups. Glass dyed purple is the cheapest — often sold as tumbled stones or beads, recognizable by round bubbles under a loupe and a too-uniform color. Synthetic (lab-grown) amethyst is chemically genuine quartz but factory-made, usually identifiable by its uniform saturation, "breadcrumb" inclusions, and blue fluorescence under shortwave UV. And then there is the disclosure problem: heat-treated low-grade amethyst is often sold as "citrine" (the burnt-orange variety), which is a legitimate treatment but should be disclosed, not passed off as natural citrine.

@@@AI_M2_PARA3_WHY_HARD@@@
Synthetic amethyst is genuinely difficult to distinguish from natural, because it is the same mineral. The differences are subtle: the way color is distributed, the type of inclusions, the fluorescence response. Glass is easier — bubbles, wrong density, soft enough to scratch. This is why amethyst testing leans heavily on color zoning and inclusions rather than a single "gotcha" test. A genuine natural amethyst shows the irregular signatures of slow geological growth; a factory-made stone shows the uniformity of weeks in a lab.

@@@AI_M3_METHODS_INTRO@@@
These seven tests are ordered from least destructive and most amethyst-specific to more general confirmation. The color zoning and inclusion checks are the headline tests — they separate most natural amethyst from glass and synthetic. The temperature and hardness tests confirm the quartz family. Price and naming read the marketplace. None is conclusive alone; together they tell a clear story.

@@@AI_M4_INTRO@@@
Amethyst's mineral profile is the foundation for every test above. Here is the quick reference, then the specifics for the three forms amethyst most commonly takes — bracelets and beads, tumbled stones, and geodes — plus the price ranges that help you spot a "too good to be true" listing.

@@@AI_M5_INTRO@@@
Most amethyst tests need only your eyes, your hands, and a $10 loupe. Two additional tools — a UV light and, for valuable geodes, a gemological lab — raise your confidence considerably.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_LOUPE@@@
A 10x jeweler's loupe is the single most useful tool for amethyst. It reveals whether the color is zoned or uniform, whether the inclusions are natural needles or synthetic "breadcrumbs," and whether there are round bubbles (glass) hiding inside. For amethyst beads and small tumbled stones, the loupe is where the answer usually lives.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_MOHS_KIT@@@
A Mohs hardness reference (or a piece of ordinary glass) confirms the quartz family on rough stones: amethyst (Mohs 7) scratches glass easily. Use only on rough or stones you do not mind marking — never on a polished bracelet or finished jewelry. For jewelry, the temperature and visual tests are safer.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_UV_LIGHT@@@
A 365nm UV flashlight is the best tool for separating synthetic from natural amethyst. Synthetic amethyst frequently shows a blue fluorescence under shortwave UV; natural amethyst is usually inert or shows a weak reddish-brown. It is a confirmation test, not a standalone verdict — combine with color zoning and inclusions.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_REFRACTOMETER@@@
A gemological refractometer confirms the quartz reading (RI 1.544) precisely. Since both natural and synthetic amethyst share this reading, a refractometer confirms "quartz" but does not alone distinguish natural from lab-grown — for that, inclusions and fluorescence matter more.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_LAB_CERT@@@
For valuable amethyst — large geodes, cathedral points, or specimens sold as fine-quality — a gemological laboratory report is the definitive answer. Labs can distinguish natural from synthetic amethyst through inclusion analysis and spectroscopy that no home test matches.

@@@AI_M7_INTRO@@@
If the tests above tell you how to read an amethyst, this section tells you how to read the seller. Amethyst is one of the most faked crystals, so the seller's transparency matters as much as the stone's properties. The five signs below consistently separate sellers who know their amethyst from those who do not.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_1_NAMING@@@
Naming transparency. The listing names the stone as "Natural Amethyst" or "Amethyst (SiO₂)" — not vague terms like "purple crystal." Treatments are disclosed: heat-treated amethyst sold as citrine, lab-created synthetic amethyst, dyed material. A seller who names amethyst correctly and discloses treatments is signaling they know the difference.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_2_ORIGIN@@@
Locality disclosure. The listing states a specific origin — Brazil, Uruguay, Zambia, or Mexico are common amethyst sources. Specific origins matter because they affect both value and appearance (Siberian and Uruguayan amethyst, for instance, are known for deeper color). "Imported" or no locality is not proof of a fake, but it is a missed signal.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_3_MINERAL_DATA@@@
Mineral data availability. The seller provides the Mohs hardness (7), specific gravity (2.65), or refractive index (1.544), or links to it. This signals a seller who understands mineralogy and lets you cross-check the home tests against the known values for quartz.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_4_RETURN_POLICY@@@
Clear return policy. A 30-day return policy lets you run the color zoning, inclusion, and UV tests at home and send the amethyst back if it fails. Sellers who move fakes resist returns; reputable sellers welcome them.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_5_REAL_PHOTOS@@@
Real photographs. The listing shows the actual amethyst piece you will receive, with its natural color zoning and inclusions visible — not identical catalog shots across hundreds of listings. Genuine amethyst varies from piece to piece; mass-produced fakes look uniform.

@@@AI_FAQ_0@@@
Run several tests together, with color zoning and the inclusion check leading. Genuine amethyst shows natural purple gradients — lighter and darker zones, often concentrated at the crystal tips — and under a 10x loupe it contains irregular inclusions (needle rutile, two-phase gas-liquid pockets) rather than round bubbles. It feels cool to the touch (quartz conducts heat well), and on rough stones it scratches glass (Mohs 7). If the color is suspiciously uniform, the inclusions look like "breadcrumbs," or it fluoresces blue under UV, it may be synthetic. A genuine natural amethyst passes most of these; a fake fails several.

@@@AI_FAQ_1@@@
The color zoning test combined with the inclusion check is the most reliable starting pair for amethyst. Natural amethyst almost always shows some color variation — subtle gradients of lighter and darker purple. Under a 10x loupe, look for needle-like inclusions, two-phase pockets, or "fingerprints" of healed fractures. Synthetic amethyst tends to be uniformly saturated with "breadcrumb" inclusions; glass shows round bubbles. Add a UV check (synthetic often fluoresces blue) and the picture is usually clear enough for a confident home verdict.

@@@AI_FAQ_2@@@
For an amethyst bracelet, start with non-destructive tests since you cannot (and should not) scratch the beads. Look at the color: do the beads show natural variation between them, or are they all an identical flat purple? Genuine amethyst beads vary slightly in tone; glass beads are often identical. Hold a bead against your inner wrist — it should feel cool and stay cool. Use a 10x loupe on a bead: natural amethyst shows irregular inclusions; glass shows round bubbles. The price is a signal too — a strand of large "amethyst" beads for a few dollars is very likely glass or dyed.

Natural amethyst, like all natural quartz, contains irregular inclusions (needles, veils, mineral flecks) or is inclusion-free, but it does not contain round air bubbles. @@@AI_FAQ_6@@@
Lab-created (synthetic) amethyst is genuinely amethyst in composition — it is SiO₂, colored by iron, with the same Mohs 7 hardness and density as natural. What it is not is natural: it was grown in a factory over weeks rather than mined after forming over geological time. The material is real; the question is disclosure. Selling synthetic amethyst as natural, without labeling it "lab-created," is misleading and against FTC guidelines. If you want a natural specimen — for the inclusions, the growth history, or the symbolic connection to geological time — ask explicitly and read the listing for "lab-created" or "synthetic."

@@@AI_FAQ_8@@@
Color change in amethyst usually points to one of three causes. First, prolonged direct sunlight can fade amethyst's purple — the color comes from iron and natural irradiation, and UV exposure can lighten it over time. Keep amethyst out of direct sun. Second, some "amethyst" sold cheaply is dyed, and dye can bleed or fade with washing or wear — look for dye concentrated in cracks under a loupe. Third, heat turns amethyst into citrine (yellow/orange) — this is how most commercial "citrine" is made, a legitimate treatment but one that should be disclosed. If your amethyst is shifting toward yellow or brown, it may have been heat-sensitive or dyed.
