This guide covers the ten crystals most commonly sold as fakes, with the mineral data and the one headline test that catches each imitation. If a stone you are considering is on this list, read its section before you buy.

For each of the ten stones below, there is one test that catches most fakes — the matrix test for turquoise, the pyrite-fleck test for lapis, the heft test for jadeite, the origin test for moldavite. Naming and price — read the listing first. Vague names ("purple crystal," "energy stone") and prices far below the species' market range are the strongest pre-purchase warnings.

Bubbles under a loupe — for any transparent stone on this list (amethyst, citrine, clear quartz, rose quartz), round bubbles mean glass.

@@@AI_M2_PARA1_MARKET@@@
The fake-crystal market is not random — it concentrates where the economics favor substitution. A stone gets heavily faked when three conditions line up: it is popular enough that demand outpaces ethical supply, it is expensive enough (or its high grades are expensive enough) that substitution is profitable, and its appearance can be approximated by glass, plastic, dye, or a cheaper mineral. The ten stones below meet all three conditions. Together they account for the great majority of fakes a buyer will encounter.

@@@AI_M2_PARA2_FAKE_TYPES@@@
The fakes follow recognizable patterns. Glass imitations dominate for transparent stones (amethyst, citrine, clear quartz, rose quartz) — dyed to match the color, often with round bubbles visible under a loupe. Plastic and resin fakes appear in opaque stones sold as cheap beads (turquoise, malachite, lapis). Dyed stones substitute a cheap mineral for an expensive one: howlite dyed blue for turquoise, jasper dyed for lapis, low-grade quartz dyed for jade. Lab-grown (synthetic) quartz is chemically genuine but factory-made. And for rare stones like moldavite, plain green bottle glass is the most common imitation, relying on buyers who do not know the origin story.

@@@AI_M2_PARA3_WHY_HARD@@@
Each stone has a specific weakness that fakes exploit and a specific test that catches the fake. Turquoise's weakness is that dyed howlite copies its color; its test is the natural matrix. Lapis's weakness is that dyed jasper copies its blue; its test is the pyrite flecks. Jadeite's weakness is its high value, inviting quartz and serpentine substitutes; its test is the density. Moldavite's weakness is its rarity and high price; its test is the Czech-only origin. The general tests from the full guide apply everywhere, but the species-specific tests are what catch the sophisticated fakes for each stone.

@@@AI_M5_INTRO@@@
The tools below cover the tests in this guide. Most are inexpensive; the lab certificate is reserved for high-value specimens.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_LOUPE@@@
A 10x jeweler's loupe is the most valuable tool across all ten stones. It reveals bubbles in glass (amethyst, citrine, clear quartz, rose quartz), dye concentrations in cracks (turquoise, lapis, malachite), the texture of pyrite in lapis, and the natural inclusions that separate genuine stones from manufactured perfection.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_MOHS_KIT@@@
A Mohs hardness reference confirms the quartz family (amethyst, citrine, clear quartz, rose quartz all at Mohs 7) by their ability to scratch glass. Use only on rough stones — never on jewelry or polished pieces.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_UV_LIGHT@@@
A 365nm UV flashlight helps with a few stones on this list: synthetic amethyst (blue fluorescence), fluorite species, and some amber. For most of the ten, it is a secondary tool rather than a primary one.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_REFRACTOMETER@@@
A gemological refractometer measures refractive index precisely, distinguishing quartz (1.544) from glass (1.50) and jadeite (1.66) from its substitutes. A professional tool — most buyers will rely on a gemologist rather than own one.

@@@AI_M5_TOOL_LAB_CERT@@@
For high-value stones on this list — jadeite bangles, moldavite over a few hundred dollars, untreated Sleeping Beauty turquoise, large fine amethyst geodes — a gemological laboratory certificate is the only definitive answer. Home tests narrow the odds; a lab report settles them.

@@@AI_M7_INTRO@@@
Across all ten of these heavily faked stones, the same seller signals separate reputable sources from those moving imitations. If you are shopping for any stone on this list, these five checks come first.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_1_NAMING@@@
Naming transparency. The listing names the actual mineral species — "Natural Turquoise," "Lapis Lazuli," "Jadeite" — not vague terms. Treatments are disclosed: dyed howlite sold as turquoise, heat-treated amethyst sold as citrine, stabilized turquoise, dyed quartz sold as jade. A seller who names stones correctly is signaling they know the difference.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_2_ORIGIN@@@
Locality disclosure. The listing states a specific origin — Afghanistan for lapis, the Czech Republic for moldavite, Iran or the American Southwest for turquoise, Myanmar for jadeite. Origin matters for these stones because it affects value and because some origins (Czech-only moldavite) are part of how you confirm authenticity.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_3_MINERAL_DATA@@@
Mineral data availability. The seller provides Mohs hardness, specific gravity, or refractive index — or links to it. Sellers who understand mineralogy bother; sellers moving fakes usually do not. The data also lets you cross-check the home tests.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_4_RETURN_POLICY@@@
Clear return policy. A 30-day return policy shifts risk to the seller and gives you time to test the stone at home. Sellers who move fakes resist returns because returned fakes expose them.

@@@AI_M7_CHECK_5_REAL_PHOTOS@@@
Real photographs of the actual piece. Natural stones vary — each turquoise bead has its own matrix, each lapis chunk its own pyrite pattern. Mass-produced fakes look identical across listings. Real photos showing natural variation are a positive sign; uniform catalog shots are not.

@@@AI_FAQ_0@@@
The most commonly faked crystals are, in rough order: amethyst (synthetic and dyed glass), citrine (heat-treated amethyst sold as natural), turquoise (dyed howlite and plastic), lapis lazuli (dyed jasper and sodalite), moldavite (green bottle glass), malachite (plastic and printed vinyl), jadeite (dyed quartz and serpentine), obsidian (industrial glass, though harder to detect), clear quartz (glass and reconstituted), and rose quartz (glass and plastic). These ten cover the great majority of fakes a buyer will encounter, because each is popular, visually copyable, and — for the high grades — valuable enough to make substitution profitable.

@@@AI_FAQ_1@@@
Apply the general tests (bubbles, temperature, hardness, price, naming) plus the one species-specific test for the stone. For turquoise, look for natural matrix and color variation. For lapis, look for real pyrite flecks. For jadeite, feel the density (it should be distinctly heavy). For moldavite, confirm Czech origin. For any transparent stone (amethyst, citrine, clear quartz, rose quartz), check for round bubbles under a 10x loupe — bubbles mean glass. Run the tests together; a genuine stone passes several, a fake fails several.

@@@AI_FAQ_2@@@
Turquoise and amethyst lead the list. Turquoise is faked constantly because genuine untreated turquoise is expensive and dyed howlite copies its color closely — the "turquoise" in much inexpensive jewelry is dyed howlite or plastic. Amethyst is faked constantly because it is the most popular crystal and synthetic amethyst (chemically identical) plus dyed glass fill the market. Moldavite deserves a special mention: because genuine moldavite is rare and has become expensive, the fake rate is extremely high — large "moldavite" pieces at low prices are almost always green bottle glass.

@@@AI_FAQ_3@@@
Yes, extremely common. Genuine untreated turquoise is relatively expensive (especially fine grades like Sleeping Beauty), and dyed howlite — a cheap white mineral that takes blue dye well — copies turquoise's color closely enough to fool casual buyers. The result is that a large share of "turquoise" in inexpensive jewelry and beads is actually dyed howlite, plastic "block turquoise" (resin with turquoise dust), or magnesite. The tests: look for natural matrix (host-rock veining, never too uniform), color variation (genuine turquoise varies; dyed howlite is uniform), and the right density (plastic feels light). A reputable seller discloses stabilization, which is legitimate, and names the origin.

@@@AI_FAQ_4@@@
Look for sellers who do three things across all these heavily faked stones: name each stone by its actual mineral species (not vague terms), disclose treatments (dyed, heat-treated, stabilized, lab-created), and state a specific origin. Beyond that, check for mineral data availability, a clear return policy, and real photographs showing natural variation between pieces. The crystal collection on this site names stones and sources precisely because these are the stones where fakes concentrate — naming and sourcing are how you verify rather than trust.

@@@AI_FAQ_5@@@
Fakes are dramatically cheaper than genuine stones, especially for high-value species. A few ratios: genuine moldavite starts around $20-80 per gram, while green-glass fakes sell for $5-20 for a large piece. Genuine untreated turquoise beads run $1-5 each, while dyed howlite beads cost cents. Genuine jadeite bangles start around $50 and fine imperial jadeite reaches thousands, while dyed-quartz "jade" bangles sell under $20. The absolute price matters less than the ratio to the species' market range — a price far below the established range for that stone and grade is the strongest pre-purchase warning.

@@@AI_FAQ_6@@@
Lab-created stones are genuine in composition (synthetic amethyst is real SiO₂), but they are not natural — they were grown in a factory rather than mined. Whether they count as "fake" depends on disclosure. Sold as "lab-created" or "synthetic," they are legitimate and often beautiful. Sold as natural without disclosure, they are misleading and against FTC guidelines. For the stones on this list — especially amethyst, citrine, and clear quartz — always check the listing for "lab-created," "synthetic," or "heat-treated," and ask the seller directly if you want a natural specimen.

@@@AI_FAQ_7@@@
Most fake crystals are not health-hazardous — they are glass, plastic, or dyed stone, which are inert. The real cost is financial and emotional: paying for something you did not receive. A few mineral-specific notes apply across this list: malachite is copper-bearing, so "malachite water" (soaking and drinking) is not safe. Dyed stones (turquoise, lapis, agate) can bleed pigment onto skin or fabric. Beyond these specific cases, the concern with fakes is honesty, not toxicity.

@@@AI_FAQ_8@@@
Home tests do not catch every fake — they catch the obvious ones. Glass sold as quartz, plastic sold as turquoise, and dyed howlite sold as lapis are easily filtered with a $10 loupe and the species-specific tests. But sophisticated imitations — well-made synthetic quartz, expertly reconstituted material, high-quality dyed stones — can pass several home tests. For valuable specimens (jadeite, moldavite, untreated turquoise, fine amethyst geodes), a gemological laboratory report is the only definitive answer. Think of home tests as a strong first filter that handles the bulk of the market, not a final verdict on expensive pieces.

@@@AI_FAQ_9@@@
Moldavite is faked so often because genuine supply is scarce and shrinking, while demand has risen sharply. Moldavite is a tektite — natural glass formed by a meteorite impact about 15 million years ago — and it is found only in the Czech Republic, in the Moldau River valley. The deposits are largely mined out, which has driven prices up steeply. That combination of high price, limited supply, and a material (green glass) that visually approximates moldavite creates a strong incentive to substitute. The result is that large "moldavite" pieces at bargain prices are almost always green bottle glass. The tests: confirm Czech origin, look for natural lechatelierite bubbles and sculpted texture, and treat price as a primary signal.

@@@AI_FAQ_10@@@
Very often. Howlite is a soft, white, porous mineral that takes blue dye exceptionally well, which makes it the most common substitute for turquoise. It is also sometimes dyed to imitate lapis or red coral. There is nothing wrong with howlite itself — it is a legitimate stone — but when dyed blue and sold as turquoise without disclosure, it is misleading. The tests: dyed howlite usually has a uniform royal-blue color (genuine turquoise varies), dark veining that looks too regular, and no genuine matrix. A reputable seller labels dyed howlite honestly rather than passing it off as turquoise.
