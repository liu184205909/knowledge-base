# Six of Swords: Tarot Card Meaning & Best Crystals for Self-Reflection

The **Six of Swords tarot** is the card of Air's passage — a woman wrapped in a shawl and a small child sitting in a flat-bottomed boat, a boatman poling them across calm water toward a distant shore, six swords standing upright in the vessel's bow. As the suit's **Passage to Calmer Water**, this is the moment the mind, after heartbreak and conflict, accepts the work of moving toward calmer understanding. On this page you'll find the full Six of Swords tarot card meaning (upright and reversed), its yes/no answer, love and career readings, the **best crystals for Six of Swords** across five roles, and an Eastern lens on the ferrying across to the farther shore.

## Six of Swords at a Glance

- **Yes / No (upright):** **Yes** — a passage yes; the card favours transition, supported movement, and the journey toward calmer water.
- **Yes / No (reversed):** **Maybe** — the passage is being resisted or stalled; the card advises honest attention to what is being clutched from the choppy shore.
- **Archetype:** The Passage to Calmer Water.
- **Core message:** transition toward steadier ground; the mind ferried with help.
- **Best crystal overall:** Aquamarine (Water, calm / courage / safe passage).

## The Suit of Swords: Air Energy

Swords is the **Air** suit — mind, intellect, communication, and truth. The suit's whole arc traces the journey of the mind: from the clean breakthrough of the Ace, through the stalemate of the Two, the heartbreak of the Three, the rest of the Four, the conflict of the Five), the passage of the Six, the strategy of the Seven, the restriction of the Eight, the anxiety of the Nine, and the completion of the Ten. The Six of Swords is the suit's **passage node** — Air moving toward steadier ground, the mind accepting the work of recovery.

## Six of Swords Upright: Meaning & Message

A woman wrapped in a shawl and a small child sit in a flat-bottomed boat; a boatman poles them across calm water toward a distant shore, and six swords stand upright in the vessel's bow — the picture of a deliberate journey from turbulence toward peace. The Six of Swords is Air's passage: the moment the mind, after heartbreak and conflict, accepts the work of moving toward calmer understanding, often with the help of another who knows the waters.

Read it slowly and the card's whole teaching appears: **the swords stand upright in the bow — the lessons (and the wounds) are being carried forward, not left behind.** Upright, this card honours genuine transition — leaving choppy shores, ferrying the lessons (and the swords) forward, regaining composure at a pace the nervous system can sustain. The figure does not travel alone; the boatman represents the help — therapist, mentor, friend, guide — that knows the waters.

Upright, the Six of Swords is one of the deck's most hopeful cards in the aftermath of difficulty. It does not promise the destination is close; it promises that *the passage has begun*, and that the direction is toward steadier ground. The card's quiet teaching is that recovery is rarely a leap — it is a slow, supported ferrying, and the willingness to board the boat (and to receive the help of the boatman) is itself the work.

## Six of Swords and the Number 6 in Tarot

Every number carries its own teaching across the four suits, and the number 6 in the Minor Arcana is the **harmony / resolution / passage** node — the moment the conflict of the 5 finds its way toward steadier ground. But the *flavour* of that resolution depends entirely on which suit it lands in.

- **Six of Wands (Fire):** public victory; recognition, the contest resolved.
- **Six of Cups (Water):** nostalgia and tender memory; the heart turned kindly toward the past.
- **Six of Swords (Air):** passage to calmer water; the mind ferried toward steadier ground.
- **Six of Pentacles (Earth):** generosity and balanced giving; the held resources offered fairly.

The Six of Swords is unmistakably its own card: the boat, the boatman, the mother and child, the six swords in the bow, the calm water toward the distant shore. Where the Six of Wands is crowned and the Six of Cups remembers, **the Six of Swords ferries** — and that ferrying is the suit's specific Air-way of carrying the number 6, the mind's harmony found through supported transition.

## Six of Swords Reversed: The Shadow Aspect

Reversed, the same passage meets resistance — unwillingness to leave the turbulence, the boat stalled mid-crossing, the baggage that refuses to be ferried, calmer waters delayed by attachment to the conflict being left. The boat is still on the water; the question is whether it is moving or stuck.

The shadow here is **not** the slowness itself (sometimes the timing genuinely requires patience). The shadow is the refusal to fully board the boat — the part of the mind that prefers the familiar turbulence to the unknown calm, the attachment to the wound that has become identity. The Six of Swords reversed invites an honest question: *what are you still carrying from the choppy shore that the boat cannot hold, and what would it mean to let it be ferried rather than clutched?* This is an invitation to reflect, not a curse — reversed, the card simply marks that the passage is being resisted, and that the resistance has its own cost.

## Six of Swords in Love, Career & Finances

### Six of Swords in Love (Upright)

In love readings, the Six of Swords signals a relationship transitioning toward calmer ground — often with help (counselling, a move, a new phase), leaving behind a turbulent chapter for steadier understanding. Real passage; honour the pace.

### Six of Swords in Love (Reversed)

Reversed in love: resistance to the transition, the relationship stalled mid-passage, or unwillingness to let go of the turbulent chapter.

### Six of Swords in Career (Upright)

In career, this card points to a transition out of a difficult role or workplace toward calmer, better-suited conditions — possibly with a guide or mentor who knows the waters.

### Six of Swords in Career (Reversed)

Reversed in career: stuck in choppy conditions, the move delayed by attachment or fear.

### Six of Swords in Finances (Upright)

For finances, the Six of Swords can signal a financial situation moving from turbulence toward stability through deliberate, supported transition. Strategic framing, not specific investment advice.

### Six of Swords in Finances (Reversed)

Reversed in finances: the stabilising transition stalled, or baggage from the past preventing calmer waters. (Not investment advice — for major financial decisions, consider qualified professional counsel.)

## Six of Swords in the Eastern Tradition

The Six of Swords resonates with the East Asian image of **渡** (*dù* — "to ferry across") and the bodhisattva who rows beings from the turbulent shore of suffering to the farther shore of peace. In the Mahayana Buddhist tradition, the bodhisattva of compassion is often depicted as a ferryman — not because the beings cannot swim but because the waters are treacherous and the help is part of the path. The Six of Swords' boatman poling the mother and child across calm water toward a distant shore is, in its own Western way, exactly this image: passage is rarely made alone, and the willingness to receive the help of the one who knows the waters is itself part of the work.

Aquamarine has its own cross-tradition lineage here — historically treasured across both Mediterranean and South Asian folk traditions as the sailor's talisman and the stone of safe passage across water, the stone of the one being ferried. The pairing is natural: a passage card meets a passage stone, and the crossing becomes a little more likely to be made calm.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the Six of Swords a yes or no card?

Upright, the Six of Swords is a passage **yes** — the card favours transition, supported movement, and the journey toward calmer water. Reversed, it leans **maybe**: the passage is being resisted or stalled.

### What does the Six of Swords mean in love?

In love, the Six of Swords signals a relationship transitioning toward calmer ground — often with help, leaving behind a turbulent chapter for steadier understanding. Reversed, watch for resistance to the transition or unwillingness to let go of the turbulent chapter.

### Is the Six of Swords a bad card?

No. The Six of Swords is a passage card, not a bad omen. Even reversed it is not a curse — it simply observes that the passage is being resisted and invites honest reflection on what is being clutched from the choppy shore.

### What is the difference between Six of Swords and Six of Cups?

Both carry the number 6 — harmony / resolution — but their *flavour* differs entirely. The Six of Swords **ferries forward toward calmer water** (the boat, the boatman, the passage); the Six of Cups **turns tenderly backward** (the sunlit town, the flowered cups, the inner child). One moves toward a new shore; the other receives the past's gift.

### Which crystal is best for Six of Swords?

**Aquamarine** is the best overall crystal for the Six of Swords — its Water-element calm and safe-passage quality match the card's ferryman archetype. For the daily willingness to arrive at a new shore, wear Moonstone.
