@@@AI_UPRIGHT@@@
In the Rider-Waite image, a woman in a flowing white gown calmly opens the jaws of a lion with her bare hands — not by force, but by a quiet, steady presence. An infinity symbol loops above her head. A wreath of flowers crowns her hair and forms a chain draped around the lion's neck and tail. Mountains rise behind them both. The lion, the symbol of raw instinct and appetite, leans into her hand rather than fighting it.

Upright, Strength is the archetype of <em>gentle power</em>. The courage it depicts is not the absence of fear but its mastery — not by suppressing the lion (which only makes it stronger) but by meeting it with enough patient presence that it chooses to soften. In everyday terms, Strength often appears when you are facing something that tempts either suppression (locking the feeling away) or explosion (letting the lion loose), and the card offers a third path: holding the feeling with awareness until it shifts.

This is also a card of self-mastery rather than other-control. The lion is within. Strength is the slow, patient work of befriending your own fear, anger, hunger, or grief — of becoming the calm hand on your own jaws.

@@@AI_REVERSED@@@
When Strength appears reversed, the same inner fortitude has gone somewhere shadowed. The familiar form is <em>self-doubt</em> — the underestimating of your own capacity, the assumption that you cannot handle what is in front of you when, in fact, you have handled harder. Its opposite is raw instinct overriding mastery — the lion off the leash, appetite or anger running the show after a long period of holding it together. A third form is burnout — the exhaustion that comes from being strong for too long without rest.

This is not a verdict of weakness. A reversed card is information for reflection. Strength reversed often asks one honest question: where have you stopped being gentle with yourself in the name of being tough — and what is the cost?

@@@AI_REVERSED_PROMPT@@@
Name one raw impulse you usually either suppress or vent — fear, anger, appetite, grief. Today, try meeting it for one minute with patient attention instead: neither pushing it away nor letting it run. Strength is built in that minute.

@@@AI_LENS_PSYCH@@@
In psychological terms, Strength maps closely onto the skill of <em>emotional regulation</em> — the capacity, well-studied in research on resilience, to meet intense feeling with awareness rather than with suppression (which bottlenecks) or explosion (which damages). The most effective regulation is not the absence of feeling but the gentle, patient attention that lets a feeling move through without taking over. Read through this lens, Strength is not asking you to be tough. It is asking you to be present — and presence, sustained, turns out to be far more powerful than force.
