@@@AI_INTRODUCTION@@@
When Justice appears in a reading, it often points to the value of an honest reckoning — weighing a situation on the evidence rather than on wish or fear, and restoring fairness where it has slipped. Card 11 of the Major Arcana, it is the archetype of truth and accountability. Paired with the right crystals, this card becomes an invitation to look clearly at what is actually true, to take responsibility where it is yours, and to act with the integrity the moment requires.

@@@AI_TLDR@@@
Justice is the card of fairness, truth, accountability, and balance. Upright, it points to an honest weighing of a situation; reversed, it often asks where dishonesty or avoided responsibility has created imbalance. The crystal most often paired with Justice is Lapis Lazuli, the stone of truth and just discernment.

@@@AI_UPRIGHT@@@
In the Rider-Waite image, a figure sits on a stone throne between two pillars, a veil behind. In the right hand a sword is held upright; in the left, a set of scales. The figure wears a red robe and a crown with a small square in the front. The sword is the clarity that cuts through illusion; the scales are the fair weighing; the square in the crown is the settled, grounded mind that can hold both.

Upright, Justice is the archetype of <em>truth honestly weighed</em>. It is not the card of punishment, despite the sword; it is the card of clear seeing — the willingness to look at a situation exactly as it is, to separate fact from fear and assumption from evidence, and to act on what is actually true. In everyday terms, Justice often appears when a decision needs to be made on the merits, when an honest accounting is overdue (toward yourself or another), or when fairness has been compromised and wants restoring.

This is also a card of accountability — and the accounting runs both ways. Justice asks you to take responsibility for what is yours, and to release what is not.

@@@AI_REVERSED@@@
When Justice appears reversed, the same honesty has slipped somewhere. The familiar form is <em>imbalance</em> — a situation that is not fair, whether to you or because of you. A second form is dishonesty — your own or another's — that is keeping the scales from settling. A third is the avoidance of responsibility, the refusal to own what you have done or left undone.

This is not a verdict against you. A reversed card is information for reflection. Justice reversed often asks one honest question: where have you stopped telling the truth about a situation — to yourself, to another — and what would shift if you started again?

@@@AI_REVERSED_PROMPT@@@
Write down one situation that feels unfair or imbalanced right now, as factually as you can — separating what you actually know from what you assume or fear. Then mark which parts of it are your responsibility to address. Justice begins with that honest sorting.

@@@AI_LENS_PSYCH@@@

Research finds that this sensitivity is not a quirk but a near-universal human trait, wired into social species because fair cooperation is what lets groups survive. Read through this lens, Justice is not asking you to be cold or calculating. It is honoring that the urge toward fairness is deeply human — and asking you to apply it as honestly to yourself as to others.

For Justice, this echo reframes the card's "weighing" as compassionate clarity rather than cold judgment — the clear seeing that lets you understand a situation's causes and act more wisely going forward.
