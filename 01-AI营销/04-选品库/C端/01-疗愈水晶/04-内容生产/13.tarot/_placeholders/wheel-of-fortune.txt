@@@AI_UPRIGHT@@@
In the Rider-Waite image, a great wheel turns in the sky, surrounded by winged figures — a sphinx seated above it, a snake descending on the left, a jackal-headed figure rising on the right. Written around the wheel's rim are the letters TARO interspersed with Hebrew letters spelling a divine name. In the four corners sit the fixed signs of the zodiac — the angel (Aquarius), the eagle (Scorpio), the lion (Leo), the bull (Taurus) — each reading from a book. The wheel turns whether anyone wills it or not.

Upright, the Wheel of Fortune is the archetype of <em>cycles</em>. It is the recognition, found across every spiritual and philosophical tradition, that nothing stays fixed — what is up will turn, what is down will rise, and the only constant is change itself. In everyday terms, the Wheel often appears at a genuine turning point: a phase completing, an unexpected opening, a shift in circumstances that you did not orchestrate and cannot fully control.

This card is neither purely lucky nor purely ominous. It simply says: the wheel is turning. The work is to meet the turn well — to act on what is within your influence and to release your grip on what is not.

@@@AI_REVERSED@@@
When the Wheel appears reversed, the relationship to change has gone somewhere difficult. The familiar form is <em>resistance</em> — clinging to a phase that has already ended, holding a position the wheel has moved past, refusing to let the turn happen. A second form is a streak where momentum feels against you — not a curse, but the natural downward arc of a cycle, asking patience rather than panic.

This is not a forecast of bad luck. A reversed card is information for reflection. The Wheel reversed often asks one honest question: what are you refusing to release — and what would change if you sorted the situation into what you can actually shape and what you must simply let turn?

@@@AI_REVERSED_PROMPT@@@
Sort one current situation into two columns: what is genuinely within your influence, and what is outside your control. Commit to acting only on the first column this week. The Wheel asks you to ride the turn, not to fight it.

@@@AI_LENS_TAROT@@@
In the Rider-Waite tradition, the Wheel of Fortune is numbered 10 and corresponds to Jupiter. It is read as the archetype of cycles, fate, and turning points — the recognition that fortune rises and falls beyond any individual's control. The four fixed signs of the zodiac in the corners, each reading a book, mark the stability that exists behind the turning: the patterns that endure even as circumstances change.

@@@AI_LENS_PSYCH@@@
In psychological terms, the Wheel maps onto the well-studied concept of <em>locus of control</em> — the distinction between what we believe we can shape (internal locus) and what we attribute to outside forces (external locus). Research consistently finds that well-being depends less on what actually happens than on the accuracy of this sorting: acting on what you can influence, and accepting what you cannot. Read through this lens, the Wheel is not fatalistic. It is asking you to sort honestly — to put your energy where it can actually move something, and to release the rest.

@@@AI_LENS_CRYSTAL@@@
As a crystal companion, Green Aventurine becomes the tangible reminder that you can meet change with openness even when you cannot control it. The Wheel reminds you that the turn is coming whether you resist or not; the stone gives you a focus for the open-handed response that riding a turn actually requires. Worn as jewelry, it is a small daily cue to stay receptive — to look for the opportunity inside the change rather than only the loss.

@@@AI_EASTERN_MAIN@@@
The Wheel resonates directly with the Eastern recognition of impermanence and cyclic existence — a central teaching across both Buddhist and Hindu traditions. Importantly, this is not fatalism: the Eastern understanding of cyclical existence is not "nothing matters, so give up," but rather "nothing is fixed, so there is freedom in how you meet what turns." The very word for the cycle of existence, <em>samsara</em>, literally means "wandering" or "going around" — the wheel. For the Wheel of Fortune, this echo reframes change not as threat but as the condition that makes any genuine freedom possible.
