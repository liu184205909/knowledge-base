@@@AI_UPRIGHT@@@
In the Rider-Waite image, a bright sun shines over a walled garden, a content child rides a white horse beneath it, arms open, a banner streaming. Sunflowers line the wall behind. The scene is wholly joyful, almost disarmingly simple after the half-lit uncertainty of The Moon. There is no hidden meaning to decode; the good is on the surface, and the surface is enough.

Upright, The Sun is the archetype of <em>joyful clarity and vitality</em>. It is the card of a season going well — of energy, optimism, success, and the simple good of being alive in a body. In everyday terms, The Sun often appears when something genuinely good is present or arriving, and the card's quiet teaching is that even joy asks to be noticed and tended, not assumed.

This is also a card of clear seeing. Where The Moon was half-lit, The Sun is full daylight — the situation is what it appears to be, and the truth is available without decoding. The confidence The Sun brings is earned by clarity, not manufactured by wishful thinking.

@@@AI_REVERSED@@@
When The Sun appears reversed, the same joy has been dimmed somewhere. The familiar form is <em>muted joy</em> — the good that is present but not being felt, the success that lands flat, the moment that should be bright and somehow is not. A second form is the shadow of too much sun: <em>ego inflation</em>, excessive optimism, confidence that has crossed from grounded into grandiose.

This is not a forecast of failure. A reversed card is information for reflection. The Sun reversed often asks one honest question: where has the joy that is actually present stopped reaching you — or where has confidence stopped being earned and started being assumed?

@@@AI_REVERSED_PROMPT@@@
Name one genuine source of joy in your life right now. Tend it today with five full minutes of real attention — not scrolling past it, not qualifying it, just letting it be good. The Sun asks you to receive what is already shining.

@@@AI_LENS_TAROT@@@
In the Rider-Waite tradition, The Sun is numbered 19 and corresponds to the Sun itself. It is read as the archetype of joy, vitality, and success — the full daylight that follows the half-lit uncertainty of The Moon, and one of the most unambiguously positive cards in the deck. The child on the white horse is sometimes read as the inner child, the part of us capable of simple, unguarded joy; the sunflowers turn toward the light, as the card asks us to.

@@@AI_LENS_PSYCH@@@
In psychological terms, The Sun maps onto the well-documented role of <em>positive affect</em> in well-being and resilience. Far from being a trivial "good mood," research consistently finds that the capacity to notice, savor, and be sustained by genuine positive emotion is a core component of flourishing — and that this capacity is a skill that can be cultivated, not merely a temperament some people are born with. Read through this lens, The Sun is not promising that everything will be easy. It is reminding you that joy is something you do — a practice of attention and savoring — and that the good present in your life asks to be actively received rather than passively expected.
